What Is Decision Fatigue, and Why Does It Matter Here?

Decision fatigue is the drop in judgment quality that happens after making too many choices in a row.

A well-known study of Israeli parole judges found that favorable rulings dropped from around 65% to nearly zero within a single decision session, then jumped back up right after a break. Judges. With training, experience, and someone's freedom on the line. If decision fatigue can bend legal judgment, it can absolutely bend your 11 PM texting judgment.

A random yes or no tool interrupts that spiral. It does not replace thinking. It replaces the twentieth re-thinking of the same three options.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is it bad to let a random generator make my decisions?

No, as long as you reserve it for low-stakes or genuinely tied choices. Using it for major life decisions like relationships or finances skips necessary research, so save it for the small stuff.

Why does a random yes or no answer feel satisfying even when I know it is random?

Psychologists link this to relief from decision fatigue. The tool does not know better than you, but it ends a loop your brain could not close on its own.

Can a yes or no generator actually predict the future?

No. It has no predictive power. It works because it removes hesitation, not because it has insight into outcomes.

How is this different from flipping a coin?

Functionally similar, but a digital tool works anywhere, tracks your history, and can add themes or custom labels a coin cannot.

Is a 50/50 tool ever more accurate than my gut instinct?

Not more accurate, but often faster with the same outcome, since research shows first-instinct choices and random choices land on similar satisfaction levels for low-stakes calls.
